# occlusion

1. # English
- *Occlusion** is a general English term derived from Latin *occludere* (“to shut, to close”). It is widely used across disciplines to describe a process of closure, blockage, or covering. Its meaning depends on the context.

1. ## Domains of use
- **Medicine/Dentistry** – the alignment and contact between teeth; or blockage of a blood vessel (vascular occlusion).
- **Dermatology/Pharmaceutics** – the covering or sealing of the skin (e.g. by a film, dressing, or formulation) to enhance absorption or to block air exchange.
- **Entomology/Parasitology** – the closure of spiracles or other openings of an arthropod, often by oils, polymers, or powders, leading to suffocation.
- **Meteorology** – an occluded front; the process by which a cold front overtakes a warm front.
- **Linguistics/Phonetics** – complete closure of the vocal tract in producing stop consonants.

1. # Dutch
The English term *occlusion* is translated into Dutch in various ways depending on the field:

- **occlusie** – general loan translation; common in medicine, dentistry, dermatology, linguistics, meteorology.
- **afsluiting** – neutral Dutch equivalent, used in medicine (e.g. *vasculaire afsluiting*), patents, parasitology.
- **verstopping** – used in everyday medical contexts (intestinal blockage).
- **afsluitend effect** – descriptive phrasing, often in pharmaceutical/patent contexts.
- **bedekking** – in dermatology, for covering the skin.
- **occlusief effect** – common in cosmetic/dermatological product descriptions.
- **occlusief verband** – medical dressing that seals the skin.
- **afsluiting (occlusie)** – bilingual form often used in patent drafting for precision.
